The History of Supreme: From a Skate Shop to a Global Icon

In 1994, a British-born entrepreneur opened a small skateboard shop on Lafayette Street in downtown Manhattan. There was no grand plan for world domination. The store was designed around skaters — open floor space so kids could ride through, staff who were part of the scene, an attitude that made it feel like a clubhouse rather than a retail store. Nobody predicted that this shop would become the most influential streetwear brand on earth. But that's exactly what happened.

This is the complete history of Supreme — how it started, how it grew, and why it still matters thirty years later.

1994 — The Lafayette Street Original

Founded 1994
James Jebbia Opens on Lafayette Street

James Jebbia had already worked in New York retail — he'd co-founded Union NYC and worked at Parachute. When he opened Supreme at 274 Lafayette Street in SoHo, he designed it specifically for skaters. The store layout was intentionally open, with product displayed around the perimeter so skaters could actually skate through the space. The staff were skaters. The vibe was intentionally not welcoming to outsiders — which, paradoxically, made insiders want it more. The brand name came from the Supreme brand of martial arts equipment, though Jebbia later acquired the trademark. The logo — white Futura Heavy Oblique text on a red rectangle — was lifted from artist Barbara Kruger's visual language, though without her permission or involvement.

From the beginning, Supreme operated on scarcity. Limited quantities, no restocks, new product dropping on a schedule that created anticipation. This wasn't a calculated marketing strategy in the modern sense — it was just how a small shop operated. But it created a culture of urgency around the brand that would define it for decades.

Late 1990s — Building the Culture

1996–1999
Skate Teams, Artists, and the Downtown Scene

Through the late '90s, Supreme built its identity around skating and downtown New York culture. The brand ran its own skate team — Supreme New York — and produced skate videos that documented the city's scene. They collaborated with artists like Damien Hirst and KAWS before either had mainstream name recognition. They put musicians, artists, and skaters on their decks and tees. The product was still primarily functional skate gear, but the cultural curation around it was already operating at a different level than any other skate brand. Supreme wasn't just selling clothes — it was selling a point of view.

Early 2000s — The Hype Begins

2000–2006
The Box Logo Becomes a Symbol

The early 2000s were when Supreme's box logo transcended skate culture and became a broader symbol of cool. Hip-hop's embrace of Supreme — artists like Nas, Jay-Z, and later Kanye West were photographed in the gear — brought it to an audience that had nothing to do with skating. Supreme opened its second location in Los Angeles in 2004. The Thursday drop ritual became codified: new product every Thursday, limited quantities, lines forming outside the store. The box logo hoodie, in particular, became a cultural object that people paid well above retail to own. This is when the resale market for Supreme began in earnest.

"Supreme wasn't the first brand to do limited drops — but it was the first to make the drop itself a cultural event."

2007–2012 — Going International

2007–2012
Japan, London, and the Global Expansion

Supreme opened in Japan in 2009 — first in Daikanyama, Tokyo, then Osaka and Nagoya. Japan was already deeply familiar with American streetwear, and Supreme found an audience that matched or exceeded the intensity of its New York following. The Japanese stores generated lines that rivaled anything in New York. A London store followed in 2011. These weren't just retail outposts — they were proof that Supreme had become a genuinely global phenomenon. The brand also deepened its collaboration strategy during this period, working with Nike on a series of Dunk SB colorways that became some of the most coveted sneaker releases in history.

2012–2017 — Peak Hype Culture

2012–2017
The Era of the Collab and the Resale Explosion

This period is when Supreme became a mainstream cultural phenomenon. Collaborations with Louis Vuitton, The North Face, Nike, Comme des Garçons, and dozens of other brands generated global media coverage. The Louis Vuitton collaboration in 2017 — shown at Paris Fashion Week — was a watershed moment: it was proof that a skate brand from downtown Manhattan had reached the same level as the most storied luxury houses in the world. Resale platforms like StockX and Grailed were built partly on Supreme's back. Lines outside the Lafayette Street store stretched for blocks. Bots were deployed to buy out online drops within seconds. The culture had fully crossed over.

2017
The Carlyle Group Investment — $500 Million Valuation

In October 2017, private equity firm The Carlyle Group acquired a 50% stake in Supreme, valuing the company at approximately $1 billion. For a brand that had started as a single skate shop with no outside investment, this was a remarkable milestone. It also marked the beginning of a new chapter — Supreme was no longer just a subculture brand, it was a significant business asset. Jebbia retained control of the brand's creative direction, but the financial stakes were now very different.

2020 — The VF Corporation Acquisition

October 2020
VF Corporation Buys Supreme for $2.1 Billion

VF Corporation — the parent company of The North Face, Vans, and Timberland — acquired Supreme in October 2020 for $2.1 billion. The deal was one of the largest in streetwear history. Many in the community worried that corporate ownership would dilute the brand's authenticity. Those concerns weren't entirely unfounded — the following years saw criticism of product quality, inconsistent collaborations, and a perception that the brand had lost some of its edge. VF itself faced financial difficulties, and Supreme's trajectory under corporate ownership became complicated.

2024 — EssilorLuxottica and a New Chapter

July 2024
EssilorLuxottica Acquires Supreme for $1.5 Billion

In July 2024, EssilorLuxottica — the Italian eyewear conglomerate behind Ray-Ban and Oakley — acquired Supreme from VF Corporation for $1.5 billion. The sale at a lower price than VF paid reflected the challenges Supreme had faced under corporate ownership. James Jebbia remained with the brand. The acquisition was notable because EssilorLuxottica is primarily a hardware company — the strategic logic centered on Supreme as a brand platform rather than a streetwear operation. What this means for Supreme's creative direction long-term remains to be seen.

What Makes Supreme's Resale Market So Durable

Thirty years after it opened, Supreme product still commands significant premiums on the resale market. The reasons are structural:

  • Genuine scarcity. Supreme has never overproduced. Limited quantities aren't a marketing trick — they're how the brand has always operated. This keeps supply below demand permanently.
  • Consistency of the box logo. The red box logo has remained essentially unchanged since 1994. It's one of the most recognizable marks in fashion, which makes authenticated pieces easy to identify and trust.
  • Collab depth. Supreme has collaborated with more brands and artists than almost any other label in history. Each collab is its own collectible universe with dedicated buyers.
  • Cultural longevity. Unlike trend-driven brands, Supreme has managed to remain culturally relevant across multiple generations of consumers. Kids who weren't born when the Lafayette store opened are standing in line for drops today.
  • Vintage appreciation. Early Supreme — pieces from the '90s and early 2000s — has become genuinely collectible, with certain items reaching prices that rival fine art in the streetwear context.

Supreme in New York Today

The original Lafayette Street store is still open. It's been joined by additional New York locations, but Lafayette remains the flagship — the place where it all started and where the lines still form every Thursday. New York has a complicated relationship with Supreme now: the brand that defined downtown cool is now a global luxury asset owned by an Italian eyewear company. But the product still ships from that same neighborhood, and the culture that built it is still very much alive.
